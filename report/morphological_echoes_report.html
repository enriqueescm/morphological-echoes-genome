From Typology to Genomics: Revisiting Human Morphological Diversity Through Population Genetics

Author
Affiliation

Enrique Estevez

MSc Bioinformatics & Biostatistics | PhD Biomedicine | Biological Anthropologist

Published

May 14, 2026

Abstract

Human morphological diversity has been documented by physical anthropologists for over a century, yet its genomic underpinnings remain incompletely understood. Using whole-genome data from 2,504 individuals across 26 globally distributed populations from the 1000 Genomes Project Phase 3, we characterize population structure and genetic differentiation across the major human superpopulations. Principal component analysis reveals clear geographic clustering consistent with known demographic history, including the Out-of-Africa bottleneck, clinal variation across Eurasia, and complex admixture patterns in the Americas. Pairwise FST analysis identifies substantial differentiation between African and East Asian populations (Hudson FST = 0.154), with the craniofacial master regulator RUNX2 showing variants in the top 1% of differentiated loci. Collagen gene COL1A1 shows low differentiation, consistent with purifying selection on structural proteins. These findings provide a genomic framework for interpreting morphological variation that transcends discrete racial typologies and situates human diversity within its proper demographic and evolutionary context.

Keywords: population genomics, craniofacial variation, FST, principal component analysis, biological anthropology, 1000 Genomes Project, RUNX2

1 Introduction

1.1 The Typological Legacy and Its Genomic Critique

The study of human morphological variation carries a complex intellectual history. From the earliest craniometric studies of the 18th and 19th centuries — Blumenbach’s racial classifications, Morton’s skull collections, Broca’s anthropometric protocols — physical anthropology long operated under a typological framework that sought to partition human diversity into discrete, bounded categories. These efforts, however well-intentioned their scientific framing, ultimately reified socially constructed racial categories rather than capturing the biological reality of human variation (Lewontin, 1972; Relethford, 2009).

The molecular revolution fundamentally challenged this typological paradigm. Lewontin’s seminal 1972 analysis demonstrated that the vast majority of human genetic variation (~85%) exists within rather than between populations — a finding repeatedly confirmed by subsequent studies using increasingly dense marker sets (Barbujani et al., 1997; Rosenberg et al., 2002). The Human Genome Project and its successors revealed a species with remarkably low genetic diversity compared to other great apes, a direct consequence of our recent common ancestry and the demographic bottlenecks associated with the Out-of-Africa dispersal (Li et al., 2008).

Yet the genomic critique of racial typology does not imply that human populations are genetically homogeneous. Substantial and biologically meaningful variation exists, structured by geography, demographic history, and natural selection. Understanding this variation — its patterns, causes, and morphological correlates — is precisely the task of a modern biological anthropology informed by genomics.

1.2 Craniofacial Variation as a Model Phenotype

The human craniofacial complex occupies a privileged position in biological anthropology, both historically and scientifically. Historically, cranial measurements formed the backbone of physical anthropological classification for over a century. Scientifically, facial morphology is among the most variable and visually salient aspects of human phenotypic diversity, making it an ideal model for studying the genetic architecture of complex traits.

Recent genome-wide association studies have identified dozens of loci influencing normal facial variation (Claes et al., 2018; White et al., 2021), with many showing signatures of population differentiation consistent with local adaptation. Key candidate genes include EDAR, which influences hair follicle morphology and facial soft tissue in East Asian populations (Kamberov et al., 2013); PAX3, associated with nasal morphology across populations (Adhikari et al., 2016); and RUNX2, a transcription factor essential for osteoblast differentiation and craniofacial ossification patterns (Lefevre et al., 2018).

1.3 Objectives

This study pursues three interconnected objectives:

  1. To characterize the genomic population structure of globally distributed human populations using principal component analysis, situating genetic clusters within their demographic and geographic context.

  2. To quantify pairwise genetic differentiation (FST) between major superpopulations, identifying which population pairs show the greatest divergence and interpreting these patterns through the lens of human demographic history.

  3. To examine whether craniofacial candidate genes on chromosome 1 — specifically RUNX2 and COL1A1 — show elevated population differentiation consistent with local adaptation or relaxed purifying selection.

2 Methods

2.1 Data Sources

Genotype data were obtained from the 1000 Genomes Project Phase 3 (1000 Genomes Project Consortium, 2015), focusing on chromosome 1 as the largest autosome and one containing key craniofacial candidate genes. The dataset comprises 2,504 unrelated individuals from 26 populations spanning five continental superpopulations: African (AFR, n=661), East Asian (EAS, n=504), European (EUR, n=503), South Asian (SAS, n=489), and Admixed American (AMR, n=347).

2.2 Bioinformatic Pipeline

All genomic analyses were performed using PLINK2 v2.0.0-a.7.1 (Chang et al., 2015) on macOS (Apple Silicon). The complete pipeline is available at github.com/enriqueescm/morphological-echoes-genome.

2.2.1 Quality Control

Raw VCF files underwent the following filtering steps:

Filter Parameter Rationale
Minor allele frequency MAF > 5% Remove rare variants with limited population-discriminating power
Allele count Biallelic SNPs only Standardize variant format for downstream analyses
Individual missingness < 10% Exclude low-quality samples
Variant missingness < 10% Exclude low-quality variants

After filtering, 530,434 SNPs were retained from an initial 6,468,094 variants scanned.

2.2.2 Linkage Disequilibrium Pruning

To obtain a set of approximately independent markers for PCA — which assumes independence among input variants — we applied LD pruning using a sliding window approach (window = 50 SNPs, step = 10, r² threshold = 0.1). This reduced the variant set to 22,415 independent SNPs, representing ~4.2% of post-QC variants. The high pruning rate (95.8% removed) reflects the extensive LD structure of chromosome 1, consistent with known recombination hotspot distributions in the human genome (Myers et al., 2005).

2.2.3 Principal Component Analysis

PCA was performed on the LD-pruned variant set using PLINK2’s implementation, computing the top 10 principal components. Eigenvectors and eigenvalues were exported for visualization in R v4.4.3 using ggplot2 v3.5.0.

2.2.4 FST Estimation

Pairwise FST between superpopulations was estimated using the Hudson method (Bhatia et al., 2013), which has been shown to produce less biased estimates than the classical Weir-Cockerham method, particularly in the presence of unequal sample sizes. Per-variant FST was calculated for all 10 pairwise population comparisons using PLINK2’s --fst report-variants option.

2.2.5 Candidate Gene Analysis

Craniofacial candidate genes on chromosome 1 were identified from the GWAS Catalog and primary literature:

Gene Genomic Coordinates (GRCh37) Function
RUNX2 chr1:45,392,147-45,746,609 Master regulator of osteoblast differentiation and craniofacial ossification
COL1A1 chr1:101,941,859-101,974,317 Encodes the alpha-1 chain of type I collagen; structural component of bone matrix

SNPs within these regions were annotated in the per-variant FST output and compared against the genome-wide FST distribution.

The dominant axis of human genetic variation (PC1, 56.85% variance explained) separates African from non-African populations with near-complete fidelity. This pattern directly reflects the Out-of-Africa dispersal: approximately 60,000-70,000 years ago, a small founding population left Africa, carrying only a subset of the genetic diversity present on the continent (Li et al., 2008). The resulting bottleneck reduced genetic diversity in non-African populations — a pattern visible here in the compressed clustering of EUR, EAS, and SAS relative to the more dispersed AFR cluster.

PC2 (24.23%) captures the major axis of within-Eurasian variation, separating East Asian from European and South Asian populations. Notably, South Asian populations (SAS) occupy an intermediate position between European and East Asian clusters, consistent with their geographic location and shared demographic history with both western and eastern Eurasian lineages (Reich et al., 2009).

Admixed American populations (AMR) show characteristic intermediate positioning across multiple PCs, reflecting their complex demographic history: indigenous American ancestry (itself derived from a subset of East Asian ancestors who crossed Beringia ~15,000-20,000 years ago), combined with substantial European and, in some populations, African admixture following the Columbian Exchange (Reich et al., 2012).

The AFR–EAS pair shows the highest FST (0.154), reflecting both the deep divergence time between African and Asian lineages and the strong demographic bottleneck experienced by East Asian populations, which is known to have amplified genetic drift and increased differentiation from the African source population (Jakobsson et al., 2008).

The EUR–SAS pair shows the lowest inter-continental FST (0.032), consistent with the shared Indo-European demographic history connecting these populations through the Pontic steppe migrations of the late Neolithic and Bronze Age (Haak et al., 2015). This genomic proximity mirrors the linguistic relatedness of Indo-European language families — a striking convergence of cultural and biological history.

The AMR–EUR FST (0.022) is the lowest of all pairwise comparisons, reflecting the substantial European admixture in the AMR populations sampled by the 1000 Genomes Project (predominantly MXL, PUR, CLM, PEL). This is not a biological signal of proximity but a demographic one — the legacy of post-Columbian European colonization.

RUNX2 harbours one variant with FST ≈ 0.60 between AFR and EAS populations, placing it in the extreme tail of the genome-wide FST distribution. This gene encodes a transcription factor essential for osteoblast differentiation and has been directly linked to variation in cranial vault morphology, mandibular shape, and facial prognathism across human populations (Lefevre et al., 2018). The highly differentiated variant likely reflects either directional selection favouring different RUNX2 regulatory variants in African versus East Asian populations, or strong genetic drift amplified by the East Asian bottleneck.

COL1A1, by contrast, shows consistently low FST values (mean FST ≈ 0.01), consistent with strong purifying selection maintaining the structural integrity of type I collagen — the primary organic component of bone matrix. This differential pattern between a regulatory gene (RUNX2) and a structural gene (COL1A1) is consistent with the emerging view that much of adaptive morphological evolution occurs through changes in gene regulation rather than protein structure (Carroll, 2008).

4 Discussion

4.1 Human Variation as a Continuum

The PCA results presented here illustrate a fundamental principle of human population genetics: genetic variation in our species is continuous and clinal, not discrete and typological. The absence of sharp boundaries between population clusters — particularly evident in the intermediate positioning of AMR and SAS populations — directly contradicts the typological frameworks that dominated physical anthropology for much of its history.

This continuity is not merely a statistical artefact of our analytical approach. It reflects the biological reality of a species with a recent common ancestor, extensive gene flow across geographic boundaries, and a demographic history characterized by serial founder effects rather than reproductive isolation (Ramachandran et al., 2005). The famous Fst values in humans (~0.10-0.15 between continental groups) are lower than those observed between subspecies in most other mammalian species — we are, genetically speaking, a remarkably homogeneous species whose visible morphological diversity has been systematically overinterpreted through a racial lens.

4.2 The RUNX2 Signal: Selection or Drift?

The identification of a highly differentiated RUNX2 variant between AFR and EAS populations raises an important question: does this reflect natural selection or genetic drift? Distinguishing between these processes requires additional analyses beyond the scope of this study — specifically, extended haplotype homozygosity (EHH) tests or composite likelihood ratio tests for selective sweeps.

However, several lines of evidence are consistent with a selective hypothesis. First, RUNX2 is a known target of positive selection in the human lineage, with its protein showing accelerated evolution compared to other primates (Bhullar et al., 2015). Second, craniofacial morphology shows clear population-level differences that correlate with environmental variables — cold-adapted populations in Northeast Asia show broader, more globular cranial vaults, potentially related to thermoregulation (Beals et al., 1984). Third, East Asian populations are known to harbour extended haplotypes at several loci involved in facial morphology, consistent with recent positive selection (Kamberov et al., 2013).

4.3 Limitations

This analysis has several important limitations that should be acknowledged:

  1. Single chromosome: All analyses are restricted to chromosome 1. While this provides proof-of-concept results, a genome-wide analysis incorporating all autosomes would substantially increase statistical power and provide a more complete picture of population structure and candidate gene differentiation.

  2. LD pruning reduces resolution: The aggressive LD pruning required for PCA (removing 95.8% of variants) eliminates many variants within candidate genes. A targeted analysis retaining all variants within candidate gene windows would provide more complete coverage of these loci.

  3. Absence of phenotypic data: This study cannot directly link genomic differentiation to morphological differences. Integrating 3D facial imaging data — as available in the UK Biobank — would allow direct genotype-phenotype association within a population genetics framework.

  4. Confounding by demographic history: High FST between populations does not necessarily imply selection. Without formal tests for selection (iHS, XP-EHH, CLR), the RUNX2 signal cannot be distinguished from drift with certainty.

4.4 Future Directions

This work establishes a foundation for several natural extensions. First, genome-wide extension incorporating all autosomes would provide substantially increased power for both population structure analysis and candidate gene identification. Second, integration of selection scan statistics — particularly iHS for within-population sweeps and XP-EHH for cross-population comparisons — would allow formal testing of the selective hypothesis at RUNX2 and other craniofacial candidates. Third, cross-referencing highly differentiated SNPs with the GWAS Catalog for craniofacial traits would identify whether population- differentiated variants have been previously associated with facial morphology in GWAS studies.

5 Conclusions

This analysis demonstrates that the integration of population genomics with the interpretive perspective of biological anthropology offers a powerful framework for understanding human morphological diversity. The dominant axes of human genetic variation mirror geographic and demographic history — the Out-of-Africa dispersal, Eurasian population structure, and the complex admixture patterns of the Americas are all legible in the first few principal components of genomic variation. Genetic differentiation between populations, while real and biologically meaningful, is modest by inter-specific standards and continuous rather than discrete.

The identification of a highly differentiated RUNX2 variant between African and East Asian populations — the most genetically divergent pair in this dataset — provides a genomic anchor for interpreting the craniofacial morphological differences documented by physical anthropologists. Whether this signal reflects selection or drift, it illustrates how candidate gene approaches can bridge the gap between population genomics and morphological anthropology.

Ultimately, the genome tells the same story as the fossil record, the archaeological record, and the linguistic record: Homo sapiens is a young, mobile, and deeply interconnected species whose morphological diversity is better understood as variation on a theme than as a collection of discrete types.
